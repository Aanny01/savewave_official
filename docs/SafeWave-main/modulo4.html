MODULE 4 - Preventing Phishing, Scams, and Cyber Fraud

Objective: Identify attempts at identity theft and online fraud. Learn to recognize suspicious emails or messages and know how to respond to them in order to protect personal information.

Definition and types of phishing

Phishing is a type of fraud in which cybercriminals impersonate trusted companies or individuals to steal sensitive information, such as passwords, bank details, or credit card numbers.

Main variants:

  • Spear phishing: Attacks targeting a specific person or organization, using personalized information (e.g., an email that appears to come from your boss).
  • Smishing: Phishing via SMS. Example: ‚ÄúYour package could not be delivered. Click here to reschedule.‚Äù
  • Vishing: Phishing by phone call. Example: ‚ÄúI am a technician from your bank, I need to confirm your details.‚Äù

Key fact: 91% of cyberattacks start with a phishing email (source: IBM Security).

Signs to identify a suspicious email or message

  1. Unusual sender:

  3. Suspicious links or files:

How to verify the authenticity of a website or sender

For emails:

  • Contact the company directly using its official website (not the links in the email).
  • Look for inconsistencies: pixelated logos, colors that differ from the official ones.

For websites:

  1. HTTPS and green padlock: Make sure the URL starts with ‚Äúhttps://‚Äù (not just ‚Äúhttp://‚Äù).
  2. Official domain: Amazon.com is real; Amazon-ofertas.com.es is fake.
  3. Tools such as Google Safe Browsing (https://transparencyreport.google.com/safe-browsing/search).

Social engineering techniques used in scams

Attackers psychologically manipulate their victims. Common tactics:

  • False authority: ‚ÄúI'm from Microsoft, your computer has a virus.‚Äù
  • Pretexting: They create a false story (‚ÄúI need your help to transfer urgent funds‚Äù).
  • Curiosity: ‚ÄúLook at these photos of you that appeared on the internet.‚Äù
  • Fear: ‚ÄúYour Netflix account will be blocked if you don't update your information NOW.‚Äù

Real case: In 2020, hackers impersonated Twitter to steal celebrity accounts and ask for Bitcoin (a $118K scam in a matter of hours).

How to respond to a fraud attempt

  1. Do not click on links or download files.
  2. Do not respond (you would be confirming that your email/phone is active).
  3. Report the attempt:
    • In emails: Use the ‚ÄúReport phishing‚Äù option in Gmail, Outlook, etc.
    • In SMS: Forward the message to 7726 (SPAM) in many countries.
  4. Alert others: If the fraud appears to be targeting your organization, notify the IT department.

If you fell for the fraud:

  • Change your passwords immediately.
  • Contact your bank if you shared financial information.

Suggested Activities:

  • Web phishing detection simulator.
  • Case study: Present three examples of emails (real or simulated) and ask participants to identify whether they are phishing and why.
