Fundamentals of Personal Digital Security

Objective: To understand what personal digital security is, its importance in everyday life, and the main risks we are exposed to as technology users.

Digital security protects our personal information in the connected world.

Definition of personal digital security and its relevance in today's world

Personal digital security refers to the set of measures, tools, knowledge, and habits that a person adopts to protect their personal information, electronic devices, and sensitive data when using digital technologies. This includes everything from using secure passwords and responsible browsing to recognizing threats such as viruses and online scams.

Why is it relevant today?

We live in an era where most of our daily activities‚Äîwork, study, relationships, finances, and entertainment‚Äîdepend on internet access. When we share photos, chat on social media, make payments, or upload information to the cloud, we leave a digital footprint that can be exploited by malicious individuals if we don't take precautions.

Digital security is no longer optional: it is a necessity to protect the integrity, privacy, freedom, and reputation of every individual.

Impact of digital security on everyday life

Digital security directly affects important aspects of our daily lives. Poor practice or failure can have real consequences in different areas:

  • Privacy: Insecure use of social networks, applications, or platforms can lead to the exposure of personal data such as address, location, family photos, or private messages. This puts the user's privacy at risk.
  • Finances: A person who does not take care of their banking information or passwords may be a victim of theft, scams, or financial fraud. One click on a fake link can give cybercriminals access to bank accounts.
  • Reputation: The unauthorized publication or dissemination of photos, messages, or personal data can damage a person's image in their academic, professional, or social environment. Damage to reputation can be long-lasting, as information on the internet can remain active for years.

Identifying the main digital risks

There are many threats in the digital environment that can affect users. The most common ones are described below:

Different types of malware and digital threats
  1. Malware: Malicious software designed to infiltrate devices without the user's consent.
    • Virus: Programs that replicate themselves and damage files or systems.
    • Trojan horses: They appear to be useful, but allow remote access to the device.
  2. Ransomware: Blocks access to files and demands payment to release them.
  3. Phishing: Consists of deceiving the person through emails, messages, or fake websites that imitate legitimate services (banks, social networks) to get them to hand over confidential data such as passwords or card numbers.
  4. Identity theft: The illegal obtaining and use of a person's personal data to impersonate them and carry out fraudulent activities such as purchases, contracts, or opening accounts.
  5. Impersonation: Occurs when someone creates fake profiles or accounts pretending to be someone else. This can be used to deceive, extort, or damage the victim's reputation.
  6. Cyberbullying: This is harassment or bullying carried out through digital means. It includes insults, threats, defamation, or the publication of offensive content.

The concepts of threat, vulnerability, and risk in the digital context

These three terms are fundamental to understanding how cyberattacks occur and the level of exposure a person has.

The relationship between threats, vulnerabilities, and risks determines security.
  • Threat: Any agent or situation that has the potential to cause harm. This could be a hacker, malicious software, a scam, or an attempt at impersonation.
  • Vulnerability: A weakness, error, or oversight that can be exploited by a threat. For example, a weak password, an outdated system, or poor privacy settings are vulnerabilities.
  • Risk: This is the possibility that a threat will exploit a vulnerability and cause a negative impact. The level of risk depends on the likelihood of an attack occurring and the damage it could cause.

Practical example: If a person uses the same password for all their accounts (vulnerability) and receives a fake email (threat) requesting a password change, there is a high risk that their information will be stolen.
